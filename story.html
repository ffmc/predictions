We are bad at
predicting the future.

A story about predictions by Francisco Cardoso

We looked at 2,129 resolved predictions from Metaculus, a platform where thousands of people put real forecasts on the record with confidence scores. Each question typically attracted between 40 and 300 forecasters, with a median around 60. High-profile questions on elections or pandemics drew several thousand. So, how bad are we exactly?

01 · Volume

Prediction obsession peaked in 2020.

Between 2015 and 2024, the volume of predictions on Metaculus grew steadily, then exploded. In 2020, a global pandemic made the future feel both more urgent and more unknowable than ever. People wanted answers, so they made guesses.

Notice how volume stayed high even after COVID faded. Once we start trying to predict, it's hard to stop.

02 · By Topic

The world changed. The questions followed.

We grouped all 2,129 questions into 10 topics using machine learning with no manual tagging. Each ridge below shows how much attention a topic attracted, year by year.

Some patterns are hard to miss. COVID-19 went from nothing to the most predicted topic on the platform in a single year, then collapsed almost as fast as it arrived. Russia / Ukraine barely registered until February 2022, then exploded overnight. World events don't build gradually they arrive, and forecasters chase them.

Then there's US Politics. No pandemic, no war, no once-in-a-generation crisis ever knocked it off the top. Year after year, it pulled in more forecasters than anything else on the planet. When something is chaotic enough to keep thousands of analysts guessing indefinitely, that says more about the subject than the forecasters.

03 · The Answer

Worse than a coin flip.

For the 1,665 yes/no questions, we scored each one simply: if the crowd's confidence was above 50% and the outcome matched, right. Otherwise, wrong.

32%
Accuracy across all boolean predictions

Out of 1,665 predictions: 537 right, 1,128 wrong. A coin flip would have done a better job.

04 · By Group

Conflict was the hardest. AI was the most honest.

Breaking it down by topic reveals clear patterns. Geopolitics and Russia / Ukraine were brutal. The crowd kept expecting conflicts and crises that quietly didn't happen.

AI / Technology was the exception. The tech community was more calibrated, possibly because they actually understood what they were predicting.

05 · Confidence

Higher confidence didn't mean higher accuracy.

Each dot is a prediction. Teal means right. Red means wrong. They're spread along a confidence axis: low on the left, high on the right.

06 · The Worst Misses

These ones really stung.

The most embarrassing failures: questions where forecasters were most certain something would happen. And it didn't. All carried the highest confidence score in the dataset.

07 · The Verdict

We're wrong even when we're sure.

Across 2,129 predictions, the crowd was wrong 68% of the time. But the sharper finding is in the confidence data: being highly confident didn't improve accuracy at all. The crowd was wrong at the same rate whether it was certain or uncertain. That's not bad luck. That's overconfidence, built in.

Geopolitics was the hardest domain by a distance. Global conflicts don't telegraph themselves, they arrive suddenly, upend everything, and resist the kind of gradual modelling that forecasters are good at. The crowd kept expecting crises that quietly didn't happen, and missed the ones that did.

And then there's US Politics, the single most predicted topic by volume, every year, without exception. Not because it's easy to call, but its volatility is magnetic. When something is chaotic enough to keep thousands of analysts guessing indefinitely, that says as much about the subject as it does about the crowd chasing it.

We know we're not great at predictions. And yet we'll keep making them, because looking ahead is crucial for who we are.
